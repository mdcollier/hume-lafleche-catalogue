British Contexts

Structured metadata, reports, and review workflows for early modern British philosophical texts.
Claim ceiling. This site presents approved source previews, catalogue materials, computational routing metadata, and review records to support scholarly inspection. It does not claim that Hume read, used, borrowed from, contacted, depended on, or was influenced by any listed text.

British Contexts gathers the project's public-safe work on early modern British philosophical materials: structured metadata, paragraph-level citation affordances, satellite review reports, audits, and future pedagogy / retrieval-design notes. It is a comparison track, kept separate from the La Flèche catalogue.

These materials support scholarly orientation and future review workflows. They do not claim that Hume read, used, borrowed from, contacted, depended on, or was influenced by any listed text.

What is public here?

Metadata records — orientation and structure, not source-use evidence.
Scholarly reports and audits — public-safe summaries of review work, not candidate proof.
Paragraph / citation metadata — citation and retrieval-design support, not full-text publication.
Future applications — design concepts, not operational tools.

English Philosophical Texts Online collaboration

Amyas Merivale's English Philosophical Texts Online provides structured text and paragraph-level metadata that can support citation, alignment, and future retrieval-design work.

The comparison corpus for this track is drawn from that English Hume-context collection. Full source texts are not republished here; paragraph metadata is treated as a citation and retrieval-design affordance, not as evidentiary proof, and public retrieval over these texts is not operational.

Structured representations and paragraph metadata

Paragraph-level metadata can help scholars cite, compare, and navigate relevant regions of texts without publishing source passages or candidate claims.

The emphasis is on citation support, alignment across editions, future retrieval design, auditability of the review workflow, and classroom / pedagogical use — not on evidence of influence.

Future application concept: conversations with philosophers

The same structured, audited representations could support future classroom tools in which students explore debates among figures such as Hume, Locke, Berkeley, and other early modern philosophers. The goal would be to ground questions and responses in citable regions of relevant texts, not to generate new evidentiary claims.

  • Not operational — no conversation or chatbot interface is published here.
  • No generated philosopher dialogue is presented as evidence.
  • Paragraph metadata may support citation and retrieval design, but does not itself establish historical contact or influence.
