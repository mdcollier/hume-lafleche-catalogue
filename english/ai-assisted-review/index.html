How AI-assisted review is used

British Contexts overviewCatalogueRelation recordsDossiersReview signal mapRetrieval matrix (experimental)

The British Contexts pipeline uses AI-assisted methods to route, test, audit, and organize candidate comparisons between passages by Hume and by earlier philosophers, so that scholars can inspect them efficiently. This page explains how that works and what it does — and does not — mean.

AI-assisted methods help route and audit candidate comparisons for scholarly review. They do not validate historical relation. This page establishes no claim of reading, source use, borrowing, contact, dependence, recall, validation, coverage, or influence.

What the pipeline is trying to see

The goal is not keyword matching. The pipeline tries to surface candidate comparisons — passage-level pairings routed for review — by attending to features that matter philosophically rather than only lexically:

  • the philosophical problem a passage engages;
  • its argumentative structure and the pressure the argument is under;
  • the conceptual vocabulary in play;
  • the specific textual locus — which Hume paragraph, and which comparison-author passage;
  • the comparison-author context the passage sits in;
  • how candidates group into review objects, and where they are not independent;
  • convergence across related candidates around a shared problem or locus.

Each surfaced pairing is a candidate comparison / review object — a structured research object for inspection, not a finding. It is a routing signal about where to look, not a claim about what is there.

Screening, routing, and diagnostics

Early screening / proposer models (first-pass proposers) can propose, promote, filter, or triage candidate comparisons — deciding what deserves closer review. These models do not approve candidates and do not validate historical relation; their output is treated as a review signal that helps allocate attention.

Every public dossier carries a collapsed routing diagnostics panel showing retrieval ranks, channels, flags, and provenance: how a candidate reached the public dossier. These are rank-order signals, not evidence scores — they explain method, not historical strength. Raw score magnitudes are not shown. Magnet-suspect flags and non-independence cautions are surfaced precisely to preserve uncertainty rather than hide it.

Convergence zones

A convergence zone is a cluster of candidate comparisons around a shared problem, vocabulary, locus, or argumentative pressure. It identifies a region for scholarly review, not a claim of reading, source use, borrowing, contact, dependence, recall, validation, coverage, or influence.

Zones organize clusters of candidate comparisons around a shared problem, locus, vocabulary, author, or routing pattern. They are review-navigation objects, not influence zones; clustering or density is not validation. Zones help a scholar see where several review objects may belong to one larger philosophical problem-space. See the convergence zones.

Interpretive layers

Public interpretation is separated into typed, provenance-marked, approval-gated layers, shown as a status-only card on each dossier: PI-approved observations, scholar reports, audit reports, AI-assisted review, and disagreement records. These are kept visibly distinct. Absence of a layer is not a negative finding; unpublished layers remain unpublished. If AI-assisted review is published later, it will be labelled separately and will not be presented as a human scholarly finding.

Frontier reasoning and audit

More capable reasoning models and review panels may be used to critique, pressure-test, and audit candidate comparisons — proposing alternative explanations, detecting disagreement, and checking claim-safety. Their role is to test and to surface structured uncertainty, not to certify. Model outputs, prompts, and internal evaluations are not published, and model agreement is never presented as a scholarly finding.

Agreement, disagreement, and audit status

Model agreement is a routing and review signal, not validation. Model disagreement is preserved as evidence about uncertainty, not resolved silently.

Agreement across models or channels may raise a candidate's priority for review. Disagreement may reveal ambiguity, noise, competing interpretations, or a weak candidate — it is not a failure of the system. The project's aim is to make uncertainty inspectable, so disagreement is preserved as part of the research object rather than smoothed into false consensus.

Human and PI review

Public interpretive claims require human / PI approval. AI-assisted review can support routing, testing, auditing, and organization, but a PI-approved observation is distinct from AI-assisted review, and the app is designed to avoid silently converting machine output into scholarly judgement. That is why mechanics, routing, zones, and interpretation are kept in separate, clearly labelled surfaces.
